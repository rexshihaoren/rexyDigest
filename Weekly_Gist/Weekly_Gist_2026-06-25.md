*   **Craig Reynolds** — EvoFlock: evolved inverse design of multi-agent motion (Paper) — 2026-06-24 — [https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25280](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25280)
    *   **TL;DR:** A method to automatically tune multi-agent simulation parameters using genetic algorithms to achieve desired emergent group behaviors, exemplified with flocking.
    *   **Takeaways:** Multi-agent model tuning is complex due to nonlinear parameter interactions.     *   **Implication for Rex Ren:** For practitioner-philosophers, this shows how to systematically manipulate the rules of a simulated world to produce intended emergent phenomena—a skill directly analogous to steering a reality-simulation.

*   **Divake Kumar, Sina Tayebati, Devashri Naik, Amanda Sofie Rios, Nilesh Ahuja, Omesh Tickoo, Ranganath Krishnan, Amit Ranjan Trivedi** — Uncertainty Quantification for Computer-Use Agents: A Benchmark across Vision-Language Models and GUI Grounding Datasets (Paper) — 2026-06-24 — [https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25760](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25760)
    *   **TL;DR:** The Argus benchmark reveals that uncertainty quantification rankings for GUI agents are stable within model classes but degrade across tiers, and calibrated conformal click regions improve spatial safety for deployment.
    *   **Takeaways:** UQ rankings transfer well across datasets for fixed models, but poorly across model classes and observable interfaces. Hidden-state and density methods are most stable for open-weight models; sampling and verbalized self-assessment win in specific regimes. Conformal prediction with calibrated UQ can shrink spatial safety regions by 40-60%, but calibration-test mismatch degrades coverage. Closed-source UQ should be reranked on the target rather than extrapolated from open-weight rankings.
    *   **Implication for Rex Ren:** Deploying GUI agents safely requires regime-aware uncertainty quantification; this benchmark changes how practitioners select UQ methods for autonomous computer-use agents.
    *   **CompositeScore (4.8) | Topics: Agent**

*   **Liwei Yu, Shuo Li, Ming Zhou, Ge Chu, Yan Guo** — Decoupling Reconnaissance and Exploitation: Measuring the Capability Boundaries of LLM-Based Web Penetration Testing (Paper) — 2026-06-24 — [https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25332](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25332)
    *   **TL;DR:** A decoupled evaluation framework reveals that LLM-based penetration testing agents can exploit vulnerabilities with up to 90% success when given accurate context, but autonomous reconnaissance plateaus at 50% recall due to parsing failures, highlighting distinct architectural strengths.
    *   **Takeaways:** Decoupling reconnaissance from exploitation isolates true agent capability and reveals a significant gap between exploit execution (90%) and autonomous discovery (50%). Multi-agent architectures excel at long-sequence interactions like deserialization; monolithic and graph-driven designs are better at short injections and access-control vulnerabilities respectively. Reconnaissance failures stem primarily from parsing unstructured telemetry, pinpointing a key bottleneck for autonomous agents. The framework provides a fine-grained benchmarking protocol for designing and evaluating next-generation offensive security agents.
    *   **Implication for Rex Ren:** Even highly capable AI agents remain brittle in autonomous information gathering—mastering the “code” of digital realities demands robust perception, not just powerful action modules.
    *   **CompositeScore (4.8) | Topics: Agent**

*   **Guangfeng Cai, Kaibing Yang, Shuo He, Yu Li, Shengtian Yang, Jiaqi Lv, Lei Feng** — Beyond Next-Observation Prediction: Agent-Authored World Modeling for Sequential Decision Making (Paper) — 2026-06-24 — [https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25421](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.25421)
    *   **TL;DR:** The paper proposes agent-authored world modeling, where training targets are synthesized from the agent's decision needs rather than next-observation prediction, leading to more effective world models for sequential decision making.
    *   **Takeaways:** Standard world modeling predicts next observations, often including irrelevant details for decision-making. Agent-Authored World Modeling lets the agent identify what it needs to understand about the environment before acting. Decision-oriented training targets are synthesized by retrieving relevant transition evidence across trajectories. Experiments show improved effectiveness over next-observation prediction across multiple environments and settings.
    *   **Implication for Rex Ren:** It demonstrates that world models can be more efficiently learned by focusing on decision-relevant dynamics, a step toward agents that better understand and manipulate their environments—key to the reality-code manipulator vision.
