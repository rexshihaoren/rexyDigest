**English**

  1. The fraction of human-level civilizations that survive to reach a posthuman stage is very close to zero.
  2. The fraction of posthuman civilizations that are interested in running ancestor-simulations is very close to zero.
  3. The fraction of all people with our kind of experiences that are living in a simulation is very close to one.

- Agent-Based Models (ABM) are computational models that simulate the interactive behaviors of autonomous agents (individuals, groups, organizations) to observe emergent macro-level social phenomena.
- They are highly useful for studying complex adaptive systems, such as urban development, economic fluctuations, or epidemic spread.
- ABM allows researchers to test hypotheses in controlled environments without conducting real-world experiments.
  - Examples: Simulating crowd behavior on evacuation routes to optimize urban planning.
  - Predicting the impact of different policies on economic growth.

**English**

- ABM can reveal non-linear effects and emergent behaviors in systems, which are difficult for traditional statistical models to capture.
- Enhances our understanding of the mechanisms of social complexity, inferring macroscopic outcomes from microscopic behaviors.

**English**

- Explores the moral considerations of inflicting suffering upon or terminating these "digital lives" within a simulation.
- This raises profound philosophical questions similar to those posed by the "are we living in a simulation" hypothesis.

## 🚧 Section 4｜偏见、控制与责任 / Bias, Control, and Responsibility
**中文**
- 模拟模型的设计者可能会无意中将自身的偏见嵌入到代理的行为规则中，导致模拟结果带有偏见。
- 讨论谁对模拟的输出及其在现实世界中的应用负责，尤其是在预测失败或导致负面后果时。
- 如何确保模拟的透明度和可解释性，以避免“黑箱”决策。

**English**
- Designers of simulation models might inadvertently embed their own biases into agent behavior rules, leading to biased simulation results.
- Discusses who is responsible for the output of simulations and their application in the real world, especially when predictions fail or lead to negative consequences.
- How to ensure the transparency and explainability of simulations to avoid "black box" decision-making.
